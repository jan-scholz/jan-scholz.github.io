It’s all Adam and Eve’s fault. They started plucking the tree of knowledge. Then came Newton, then Einstein. Now, what is there left to know? It seems to become more difficult to achieve impactfull breakthrough scientific discoveries. More and better educated people are needed, equipped with machines of ever-increasing complexity and price to detect the smallest of effects after the longest of studies.

Studies in the recent edition of the Nature/Science magazines are struggling to prove their importance beyond the tightly-knit circle of specialized scientists from which they originate.

quotes

From industries we know that subsidies can have adverse effects and might actually prevent a stated aim from being reached. A classic example is 'defense' R&D which has run over budget more than one time. In fact, the cost increase of fighter jets is so consistent that a law that describes its exponential increase has been invented: [Augustine's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustine%27s_laws).

<!-- CSV -->

The subsidized industry that was just geared to develop a new jet to sell and profit from it is now more interested in keeping the free money spigot open, often goading the responsible politicians into approving even more money, because the targets were not quite achieved. But every penny dropped into this mess confirms ones again that there is more money to be extracted if no progress is made.

Is the same thing happening to academic research? Here’s a good question: Do more PhDs necessarily discover more fruitful research topics? If that’s not the case, then what are they all doing? Well they sure as hell keep busy, if you’ve ever looked into a PhD/PostDoc’s sun-deprived sad mockery of a face.
