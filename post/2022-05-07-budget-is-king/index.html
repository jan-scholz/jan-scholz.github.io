Budget Is King

One thing to rule them all and in the darkness bind them.
May 7, 2022 · 2 min · Jan Scholz

The budget allocated for a project, team, or division is probably the single most important factor determining the fate of everyone involved. It is thus surprising that it is usually guarded as a secrete and shared only amongst a select few. The budget is often nebulously referred to as something one needn’t worry about during good times. When circumstances change, the budget suddenly becomes the reason for letting people go or closing down entire divisions.

The intransparency around an organization’s financial situation squanders a lot of potential to steer a company back from the precipice. Believe it or not, but most employees don’t want to lose their job and are prepared to help an organization to regain its footing during hard times. But that is exactly when leadership starts to become very secretive about “the numbers”. At the same time, nothing abuses peoples trust more than laying them off due to a “sudden” budget shortfall.

The question is then: Why don’t we share what determines people’s fates with them? Empower them to make a difference and benefit in the best case and at least give them some semblance of control in the worst case.

The budget also has a function as a management tool, in addition to its role in determining the growth (or abandonment) of projects and teams. It can help hold people and teams accountable and ensure that generated value warrants the investment. It is thus surprising of seldom budgets are used in that way. In some organizations you’d have to make it to a VP level to get your hands on the purse strings.

Budgets can provide clarity as to the cost of a project or team and give the team autonomy to invest into those resources that they know best will provide the most value. Without budget control the team will lose the feedback mechanism and instead operate outside the budget that the organization can provide or not invest aggressively enough despite the funds being available.

Of course, budget autonomy requires trust. So not giving teams or certain classes of employees access to a budget shows a lack of trust, which in turn can result in unhappy employees and lower retention rates.
